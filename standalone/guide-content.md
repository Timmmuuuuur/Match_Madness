## 1. Pronunciation & Sound System

French pronunciation is one of the first walls learners hit. The good news: it is **consistent** — once you know the rules, you can read almost anything aloud correctly.

### Vowels

| Sound | Example | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| /a/ | *chat* (cat) | Like English "ah" |
| /e/ | *été* (summer) | Like English "ay" but pure, no glide |
| /ɛ/ | *fête*, *lait* | Like English "eh" |
| /i/ | *vie* (life) | Like English "ee" |
| /o/ | *eau*, *beau* | Like English "oh" but pure |
| /ɔ/ | *or* (gold) | Like English "aw" |
| /u/ | *vous* (you) | Like English "oo" — lips very rounded |
| /y/ | *tu* (you informal) | **No English equivalent.** Shape lips for "oo", say "ee". 🇷🇺 Like the Russian «ю» minus the initial glide |
| /ø/ | *feu* (fire) | Shape lips for "o", say "e" |
| /œ/ | *peur* (fear) | Like /ø/ but more open |

### Nasal Vowels — Unique to French

These are vowels where air passes through the nose. No consonant "n" or "m" is pronounced.

| Spelling | Sound | Example |
|---------|-------|---------|
| -an, -en, -am, -em | /ɑ̃/ | *dans, enfant, temps* |
| -in, -ain, -im | /ɛ̃/ | *vin, main, simple* |
| -on, -om | /ɔ̃/ | *bon, nom* |
| -un, -um | /œ̃/ | *un, parfum* (merging with /ɛ̃/ in modern speech) |

> 🇷🇺 Russian has no nasal vowels — this requires conscious practice. The nasal quality comes from lowering the velum, not from pronouncing an "n".

### The French R

The French /R/ is a uvular fricative — made at the back of the throat, not with the tongue tip.

> 🇷🇺 It is **not** the Russian "р". Think of it as gargling gently. The closest approximation for a Russian speaker is the sound in clearing your throat softly.

### Silent Letters & Final Consonants

This is crucial. French has massive spelling-pronunciation divergence because of historical silent letters.

**Rule: Most final consonants are silent.** Exceptions: C, R, F, L (remember: **CaReFuL**).

| Written | Pronounced | Not pronounced |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| *vous parlez* | /vu paʁle/ | final -z, -s silent |
| *grand* | /gʁɑ̃/ | final -d silent |
| *chaud* | /ʃo/ | final -d silent |
| *chef* | /ʃɛf/ | -f pronounced (CaReFuL) |
| *mer* | /mɛʁ/ | -r pronounced (CaReFuL) |

### Liaison

When a word ending in a normally-silent consonant is followed by a word beginning with a vowel, the consonant is **pronounced** and links to the next word:

- *les enfants* → /le‿zɑ̃fɑ̃/ (the z links)
- *nous avons* → /nu‿zavɔ̃/
- *vous êtes* → /vu‿zɛt/

Liaison is **obligatory** after determiners, pronouns, and auxiliaries; **optional** in careful speech elsewhere; and **forbidden** before aspirated h.

### Elision

The final vowel of short words (le, la, je, me, te, se, de, ne, que) drops before a vowel or mute h:

- *le + ami* → *l'ami*
- *je + ai* → *j'ai*
- *ne + est* → *n'est*

### Accent Marks

| Mark | Name | Effect |
|------|------|--------|
| é | accent aigu | /e/ — closed e |
| è, ê | accent grave/circonflexe | /ɛ/ — open e |
| â, ô | circumflex | historically long vowel; now mostly signals open quality |
| ç | cédille | s sound before a, o, u (*garçon*) |
| ë, ï | tréma | vowel is pronounced separately (*Noël*, *naïf*) |

## 2. Nouns & Grammatical Gender

All French nouns are either **masculine** or **feminine**. There is no neuter.

> 🇷🇺 **Good news:** You already handle grammatical gender in Russian — and Russian has *three* genders. French is actually simpler: just two.  
> 🇬🇧 English has no grammatical gender, so this is a new concept for English-only speakers.

### Patterns to Guess Gender

These are tendencies, not ironclad rules:

**Usually Masculine:**
- Nouns ending in: -age, -ment, -eur (agent nouns), -eau, -isme, -ier
- Days, months, seasons, languages, most trees
- *le fromage, le gouvernement, le chapeau, le boulanger*

**Usually Feminine:**
- Nouns ending in: -tion, -sion, -ure, -ette, -elle, -ance, -ence, -té, -ière
- Most fruits, academic subjects (except math? No, *les mathématiques* is feminine)
- *la nation, la voiture, la liberté, la boulangère*

**Tricky ones to memorize:**
- *le problème* (m) — ends in -e but masculine
- *la mer* (f) — sea
- *le silence* (m)
- *la page* (f)

> **Strategy:** Learn every noun with its article — *un/le livre* not just *livre*. This is standard practice.

### Plural Formation

Add **-s** (usually silent) to most nouns:
- *un chat → des chats*

Special cases:
- -eau → -eaux: *le chapeau → les chapeaux*
- -al → -aux: *le cheval → les chevaux* (horse)
- -s, -x, -z: unchanged in plural

## 3. Articles — The Big New Concept

> 🇷🇺 **Russian has no articles at all.** This is the single biggest structural difference. You will need to develop an intuition for *when* to use which article — this takes time.  
> 🇬🇧 English has *a/an* (indefinite) and *the* (definite). French has those **plus** partitives.

### Definite Articles (the)

| | Masculine | Feminine | Before vowel/mute h | Plural |
|--|-----------|----------|---------------------|--------|
| | **le** | **la** | **l'** | **les** |

*le livre, la table, l'ami, l'heure, les enfants*

Use definite articles:
- For specific known things: *Le chat de Marie* (Marie's cat)
- For general statements/categories: *J'aime le café* (I like coffee — in general)
- With continents, countries, regions: *la France, le Japon*
- With body parts (where English uses possessives): *Je me lave les mains* (I wash my hands)

### Indefinite Articles (a/an, some)

| | Masculine | Feminine | Plural |
|--|-----------|----------|--------|
| | **un** | **une** | **des** |

*un livre, une table, des enfants*

Use for non-specific items: *J'ai un chat* (I have a cat — any cat).

> After negation, **un/une/des → de (d')**: *Je n'ai pas de chat.*

### Partitive Articles (some — uncountable)

These express an indefinite quantity of something uncountable:

| | Masculine | Feminine | Before vowel |
|--|-----------|----------|--------------|
| | **du** (= de + le) | **de la** | **de l'** |

*Je mange du pain.* (I eat [some] bread.)  
*Elle boit de la bière.* (She drinks [some] beer.)  
*Il boit de l'eau.* (He drinks [some] water.)

> After negation: *Je ne mange pas de pain.*

> 🇷🇺 The partitive concept maps roughly onto the Russian genitive of quantity (*выпить воды, съесть хлеба*) — but in French it's obligatory with an article, not just a case ending.

### Article Omission

Articles are **dropped**:
- After *être* with professions (informal): *Il est médecin.* (He is a doctor.)
- In lists: *J'aime musique, sport, cuisine.*
- After *en* with feminine countries: *en France, en Italie* (but: *au Japon*)

## 4. Adjectives — Agreement & Position

### Agreement

French adjectives agree with the noun in **gender and number**. 

> 🇷🇺 Same principle as Russian adjectives. The mechanism is simpler in French (just -e for feminine, -s for plural, -es for feminine plural).

| | Masculine sg | Feminine sg | Masc pl | Fem pl |
|--|-------------|------------|---------|--------|
| *grand* (tall) | grand | grande | grands | grandes |
| *petit* (small) | petit | petite | petits | petites |
| *heureux* (happy) | heureux | heureuse | heureux | heureuses |
| *beau* (beautiful) | beau | belle | beaux | belles |
| *nouveau* | nouveau | nouvelle | nouveaux | nouvelles |
| *vieux* | vieux | vieille | vieux | vieilles |

Note: *beau/bel, nouveau/nouvel, vieux/vieil* before masculine nouns starting with a vowel: *un bel homme, un nouvel ami*.

### Position — BANGS Rule

Most French adjectives come **after** the noun. Only a small set of common adjectives come **before**:

> **BANGS** — Beauty, Age, Number, Goodness/Badness, Size

| Before noun | After noun |
|------------|-----------|
| *beau/belle* | *rouge, bleu...* (colors) |
| *vieux/vieille, jeune* | *français, américain...* (nationality) |
| *premier, dernier, autre* | *médical, industriel...* (derived adjectives) |
| *bon/mauvais* | *intéressant, difficile...* |
| *grand/petit, gros* | *rond, carré...* (shape) |

Examples:
- *un grand homme* — a great man (figuratively)
- *un homme grand* — a tall man (physically)
- *une vieille voiture rouge* — an old red car (vieille before, rouge after)

> 🇷🇺 In Russian adjectives typically precede the noun. French adjective-after-noun is a key habit to build.

## 5. Personal Pronouns

### Subject Pronouns

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|---------|--------|
| 1st | **je** (j' before vowel) | **nous** |
| 2nd | **tu** (informal) | **vous** (formal or plural) |
| 3rd m | **il** | **ils** |
| 3rd f | **elle** | **elles** |
| 3rd neutral | **on** (one/we, informal) | — |

> 🇷🇺 French has no pronoun-dropping (*pro-drop*). You **must** always state the subject — unlike Russian where *говорю* alone is fine without *я*.

> **On** is extremely common in spoken French as informal "we": *On va au cinéma* = *Nous allons au cinéma* (We're going to the cinema).

### Stress/Tonic Pronouns

Used after prepositions, for emphasis, after *c'est*:

*moi, toi, lui, elle, nous, vous, eux, elles*

- *C'est moi.* — It's me.
- *Il parle avec elle.* — He talks with her.
- *Moi, je préfère le café.* — Me, I prefer coffee. (emphasis)

## 6. Verbs — Groups & Conjugation

French verbs are grouped by their infinitive ending:

| Group | Ending | Example | Notes |
|-------|--------|---------|-------|
| 1st | -er | *parler* | ~90% of verbs; most new/borrowed verbs |
| 2nd | -ir | *finir* | Regular -ir verbs |
| 3rd | -ir, -re, -oir | *partir, vendre, voir* | Irregular; must learn individually |

### 1st Group: -ER Verbs (parler — to speak)

**Present tense:**

| | Singular | Plural |
|-|---------|--------|
| 1st | je parl**e** | nous parl**ons** |
| 2nd | tu parl**es** | vous parl**ez** |
| 3rd | il/elle parl**e** | ils/elles parl**ent** |

The **-e, -es, -e, -ent** endings are silent. Only **-ons** and **-ez** are audibly distinct.

### 2nd Group: -IR Verbs (finir — to finish)

| | Singular | Plural |
|-|---------|--------|
| 1st | je fin**is** | nous fin**issons** |
| 2nd | tu fin**is** | vous fin**issez** |
| 3rd | il fin**it** | ils fin**issent** |

### 3rd Group: -RE Verbs (vendre — to sell)

| | Singular | Plural |
|-|---------|--------|
| 1st | je vend**s** | nous vend**ons** |
| 2nd | tu vend**s** | vous vend**ez** |
| 3rd | il vend | ils vend**ent** |

## 8. The Key Tenses

### Présent — Present

Already covered above. Used for:
- Current actions: *Je mange.* (I am eating / I eat)
- Habitual actions: *Je lis chaque matin.* (I read every morning)
- Near future (common): *On part demain.* (We're leaving tomorrow)

> 🇬🇧 One French present = both "I eat" and "I am eating". No separate progressive.

### Futur Proche — Near Future (going to)

**Aller (present) + infinitive** — extremely common in speech:

*Je vais manger.* — I'm going to eat.  
*Il va pleuvoir.* — It's going to rain.  
*Nous allons partir.* — We're going to leave.

### Futur Simple — Simple Future (will)

Formed by adding future endings to the infinitive (-er/-ir verbs) or a special stem (-re verbs drop the -e):

| | Endings |
|--|--------|
| je | -**ai** |
| tu | -**as** |
| il/elle | -**a** |
| nous | -**ons** |
| vous | -**ez** |
| ils/elles | -**ont** |

*parler → je parlerai, tu parleras, il parlera...*  
*finir → je finirai...*  
*vendre → je vendrai...*

Irregular stems: *être → ser-, avoir → aur-, aller → ir-, faire → fer-, venir → viendr-, voir → verr-, pouvoir → pourr-*

### Conditionnel — Conditional (would)

Same stem as futur simple + imperfect endings (-ais, -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient):

*je parlerais* — I would speak  
*il ferait* — he would do  

Used for: polite requests (*Je voudrais...*), hypothetical situations (*Si j'avais...je ferais...*).

### Impératif — Imperative (commands)

Use the **tu, nous, vous** forms of present tense, dropping the subject pronoun. For -er verbs, drop the -s in tu form:

| | tu | nous | vous |
|--|--|--|--|
| *parler* | Parle ! | Parlons ! | Parlez ! |
| *finir* | Finis ! | Finissons ! | Finissez ! |
| *être* | Sois ! | Soyons ! | Soyez ! |
| *avoir* | Aie ! | Ayons ! | Ayez ! |

## 9. Passé Composé vs Imparfait — The Core Tension

This is where most learners struggle. Both are "past tenses" but they do fundamentally different things.

### Passé Composé — Completed Past Actions

**Auxiliary (avoir or être) + past participle**

Used for:
- A completed action with a defined endpoint: *J'ai mangé une pomme.* (I ate an apple.)
- A sequence of events: *Il est entré, il a vu, il a conquis.*
- A sudden or punctual event in a narrative: *Soudain, le téléphone a sonné.*

Past participle formation:
- -er verbs: *parl**é**, mang**é***
- -ir verbs: *fin**i**, part**i***
- -re verbs: *vend**u**, attend**u***
- Irregulars: *être → été, avoir → eu, faire → fait, voir → vu, prendre → pris, venir → venu*

### Imparfait — Ongoing/Habitual Past

Formed from the **nous-present stem** + endings: *-ais, -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient*

*parler → nous parlons → je parlais, tu parlais, il parlait...*

Used for:
- Background descriptions: *Il faisait beau.* (The weather was nice.)
- Ongoing states in the past: *Elle était fatiguée.* (She was tired.)
- Habitual past actions: *Quand j'étais enfant, je jouais au foot.* (When I was a child, I used to play football.)
- Interrupted actions: *Je lisais quand il est arrivé.* (I was reading when he arrived.)

> 🇷🇺 This maps onto the Russian **imperfective/perfective aspect** distinction, though not perfectly. Passé composé ≈ perfective aspect (completed, result-focused). Imparfait ≈ imperfective aspect (process, state, habitual). A Russian-speaker's instinct here is often correct.

> 🇬🇧 English uses "I was doing" (progressive) for the imparfait background role, and "I did" (simple past) for the passé composé. French collapses this differently.

**Contrast in context:**

> *Hier, je **lisais** un livre quand mon ami **a téléphoné**.*  
> Yesterday, I **was reading** a book when my friend **called**.  
> (*lisais* = ongoing background; *a téléphoné* = sudden completed event)

## 10. Avoir vs Être as Auxiliaries

In passé composé, most verbs use **avoir**. About 17 common verbs use **être** — mostly verbs of motion and change of state. A mnemonic: **DR. & MRS. VANDERTRAMP**

| Verb | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **D**evenir | to become |
| **R**evenir | to come back |
| **M**onter | to go up |
| **R**ester | to stay |
| **S**ortir | to go out |
| **V**enir | to come |
| **A**ller | to go |
| **N**aître | to be born |
| **D**escendre | to go down |
| **E**ntrer | to enter |
| **R**entrer | to return home |
| **T**omber | to fall |
| **R**etourner | to return |
| **A**rriver | to arrive |
| **M**ourir | to die |
| **P**artir | to leave |

**+** all **reflexive verbs** use être.

**Agreement rule:** When using être, the past participle agrees with the subject like an adjective:

*Elle est **partie**.* (She left — feminine -e)  
*Ils sont **partis**.* (They left — masculine plural -s)  
*Elles sont **parties**.* (They [f] left — feminine plural -es)

With avoir, the past participle is invariable **unless** a direct object precedes the verb:

*J'ai vu Marie.* (no agreement)  
*Je l'ai **vue**.* (agreement because *la/l'* precedes — she was seen)

## 11. Negation

Standard negation wraps the verb with **ne...pas**:

*Je ne parle pas.* — I don't speak.  
*Il ne mange pas.* — He doesn't eat.

In compound tenses, ne...pas wraps the auxiliary:

*Je n'ai pas mangé.* — I didn't eat.  
*Elle n'est pas venue.* — She didn't come.

### Other Negation Patterns

| Expression | Meaning | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| ne...jamais | never | *Je ne fume jamais.* |
| ne...rien | nothing | *Je ne vois rien.* |
| ne...personne | nobody | *Je ne connais personne.* |
| ne...plus | no more/no longer | *Il ne travaille plus.* |
| ne...que | only | *Je n'ai que dix euros.* |
| ne...ni...ni | neither...nor | *Je ne bois ni café ni thé.* |

### Spoken French: Dropping "ne"

In informal spoken French, **ne is commonly dropped**:

*Je sais pas.* (= Je ne sais pas) — I don't know.  
*T'as pas vu mes clés ?* — You haven't seen my keys?  
*Y'a personne.* (= Il n'y a personne) — There's no one.

> 🇷🇺 This is like colloquial Russian dropping formal particles. Written and formal French always keeps the *ne*.

## 12. Questions

Three main ways to ask questions in French:

### 1. Intonation (most common in speech)

Raise your voice at the end of a declarative sentence:

*Tu parles français ?* — You speak French?  
*Il est là ?* — Is he there?

### 2. Est-ce que (neutral, all registers)

Add *est-ce que* before the subject:

*Est-ce que tu parles français ?*  
*Est-ce qu'il est là ?*

### 3. Inversion (formal/written)

Invert subject and verb, hyphenated:

*Parles-tu français ?*  
*Mangez-vous de la viande ?*

With *il/elle/on* and a verb ending in a vowel, add *-t-*:

*A-t-il faim ?* (Is he hungry?) — *a* ends in vowel, so add -t-

### Question Words

| Question word | Meaning |
|--------------|---------|
| *qui* | who |
| *que / qu'est-ce que* | what (object) |
| *qu'est-ce qui* | what (subject) |
| *où* | where |
| *quand* | when |
| *comment* | how |
| *pourquoi* | why |
| *combien* | how much/many |
| *quel/quelle* | which/what (adj) |
| *lequel/laquelle* | which one (pronoun) |

*Où habitez-vous ?* / *Vous habitez où ?*  
*Pourquoi est-ce qu'il pleure ?*  
*Combien ça coûte ?*  
*Quel film tu veux regarder ?*

## 13. Prepositions & Contractions

### Core Prepositions

| French | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| *à* | at, to, in | *aller à Paris, être à la maison* |
| *de* | of, from, about | *venir de Paris, parler de politique* |
| *en* | in, to (+ f. countries), by | *en France, en voiture, en été* |
| *dans* | in (inside) | *dans la voiture, dans une heure* |
| *sur* | on | *sur la table* |
| *sous* | under | *sous la table* |
| *pour* | for | *un cadeau pour toi* |
| *par* | by, through | *par la fenêtre, deux fois par jour* |
| *avec* | with | *avec plaisir* |
| *sans* | without | *sans sucre* |
| *avant* | before | *avant midi* |
| *après* | after | *après le dîner* |
| *chez* | at/to someone's place | *chez moi, chez le médecin* |
| *entre* | between | *entre toi et moi* |

### Mandatory Contractions

When **à** or **de** precedes **le** or **les**, they must contract:

| | *le* | *les* |
|--|------|------|
| *à +* | **au** | **aux** |
| *de +* | **du** | **des** |

*Je vais au marché.* (à + le)  
*Il parle aux enfants.* (à + les)  
*C'est le titre du livre.* (de + le)  
*Il vient des États-Unis.* (de + les)

No contraction with *la* or *l'*: *à la gare, de l'eau*.

### Countries & Cities

- Feminine countries (most ending in -e): *en France, en Espagne, en Allemagne*
- Masculine countries: *au Japon, au Canada, aux États-Unis*
- Cities: *à Paris, à New York, à Rome*

## 14. Object Pronouns & Word Order

### Direct Object Pronouns (replace the direct object)

| Person | Pronoun |
|--------|---------|
| me (m') | me |
| te (t') | you |
| le (l') | him/it (m) |
| la (l') | her/it (f) |
| nous | us |
| vous | you |
| les | them |

*Je vois Marie → Je **la** vois.*  
*Il mange le pain → Il **le** mange.*

### Indirect Object Pronouns (replace à + person)

| Person | Pronoun |
|--------|---------|
| me (m') | to me |
| te (t') | to you |
| lui | to him/her |
| nous | to us |
| vous | to you |
| leur | to them |

*Je parle à Marie → Je **lui** parle.*  
*Il donne le livre aux enfants → Il **leur** donne le livre.*

### Word Order

Object pronouns go **before the verb** (before the auxiliary in compound tenses):

*Je **le** vois.* — I see him.  
*Je **l'**ai vu.* — I saw him.  
*Ne **le** mange pas !* — Don't eat it!

> 🇷🇺 Object pronouns in French are **clitic** — they must attach to the verb and cannot move freely. Russian allows freer word order for emphasis. In French, the position is fixed.

### Double Object Pronouns

When both direct and indirect pronouns appear, order is:

**me/te/nous/vous → le/la/les → lui/leur → y → en**

*Il me le donne.* — He gives it to me. (me before le)  
*Je le lui donne.* — I give it to him. (le before lui)

### Y and En

**y** = there / to it (replaces *à + place/thing*):  
*Tu vas à Paris ? → Tu y vas ?*

**en** = some of it / from there (replaces *de + noun* or a partitive):  
*Tu veux du café ? → Tu en veux ?*  
*Il vient de Paris → Il en vient.*

## 15. Relative Pronouns: qui, que, dont, où

### qui — who/which (subject of the relative clause)

*L'homme **qui** parle est mon père.* — The man who is speaking is my father.  
*Le livre **qui** est sur la table est intéressant.*

### que (qu') — whom/which (direct object of the relative clause)

*L'homme **que** je vois est mon père.* — The man (whom) I see is my father.  
*Le livre **que** tu lis est excellent.*

> Tip: **qui** is followed by a verb; **que** is followed by a subject + verb.

### dont — of which/whose/about which (replaces *de + noun*)

*Le livre **dont** je parle.* — The book I'm talking about (about which I'm talking).  
*L'homme **dont** je connais la femme.* — The man whose wife I know.

### où — where/when (replaces place or time reference)

*La ville **où** j'habite.* — The city where I live.  
*Le jour **où** il est né.* — The day when he was born.

## 16. The Subjunctive

The subjunctive (*subjonctif*) is a mood (not a tense) that expresses subjectivity — doubt, emotion, necessity, possibility, volition.

> 🇷🇺 Russian uses *чтобы + past tense* for many of the same contexts. The subjunctive in French is the equivalent mechanism — obligatory in its triggers.

### When to Use It

The subjunctive follows specific trigger expressions. Key triggers:

**Necessity / obligation:**
- *il faut que* — it is necessary that
- *il est nécessaire que*

**Volition / desire:**
- *vouloir que* — to want (someone to do)
- *préférer que, souhaiter que*

**Emotion:**
- *être content/heureux/triste que*
- *avoir peur que*

**Doubt / possibility:**
- *douter que, ne pas croire que*
- *il est possible/impossible que*

**After certain conjunctions:**
- *bien que* (although), *pour que* (so that), *avant que* (before), *à moins que* (unless), *quoique* (although)

### Formation

From the **ils-present stem** + subjunctive endings: *-e, -es, -e, -ions, -iez, -ent*

*parler → ils parlent → que je parle, que tu parles, qu'il parle, que nous parlions...*  
*finir → ils finissent → que je finisse...*  
*vendre → ils vendent → que je vende...*

Irregular subjunctives:
- *être → que je sois, que tu sois, qu'il soit...*
- *avoir → que j'aie, que tu aies, qu'il ait...*
- *aller → que j'aille, que tu ailles, qu'il aille, que nous allions...*
- *faire → que je fasse...*
- *pouvoir → que je puisse...*
- *vouloir → que je veuille...*

**In practice:**

*Je veux qu'il **vienne**.* — I want him to come.  
*Il faut que tu **fasses** tes devoirs.* — You need to do your homework.  
*Bien qu'il **soit** fatigué, il travaille.* — Although he is tired, he works.

> If the subject is the same in both clauses, use **de + infinitive** instead:  
> *Je veux **venir**.* (not: *Je veux que je vienne.*)

## 17. Tu vs Vous — Formality & Social Register

| | *tu* | *vous* |
|--|------|--------|
| Use with | friends, family, children, peers you know well, animals | strangers, people older than you, professional contexts, plural "you" |
| Verb form | 2nd person singular | 2nd person plural form |
| Switch | *On se tutoie ?* (Shall we use tu?) | Default in formal contexts |

The social rules are real and violation causes genuine awkwardness:
- **Too much *vous*:** Can feel cold or distancing among peers
- **Too much *tu*:** Presumptuous or rude with strangers/elders

In France, the workplace is gradually shifting toward *tu* in many industries (tech, startups) — but traditional sectors (law, government, finance) still default to *vous*.

> 🇷🇺 Russian has the same distinction: ты/вы. Apply the same social logic — it maps very closely.

## 18. Spoken vs Written French

The gap between written/formal French and everyday spoken French is **enormous** — larger than in most European languages.

| Feature | Written/Formal | Spoken (informal) |
|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Negation | *Je ne sais pas* | *Je sais pas* / *Chais pas* |
| Subject | *nous allons* | *on va* |
| Questions | *Est-ce que tu viens ?* / inversion | *Tu viens ?* |
| Omission | *Il y a* | *Y'a* |
| *tu* forms | *tu as* | *t'as* |
| *ce* | *c'est* | *c'est* (same), but *cela* → *ça* always |
| Deletion | *je* before consonant | *J'sais pas → Chais pas* |

### Key Informal Patterns

**"C'est quoi ça ?"** (What's that?) — informal for *Qu'est-ce que c'est ?*

**"T'as fait quoi ?"** — Informal for *Qu'est-ce que tu as fait ?* (What did you do?)

**"On se casse ?"** — Shall we leave? (Slang: *se casser* = to split/leave)

**"Ouais"** — Yeah (*oui* is formal "yes"; *ouais* is everyday)

**"Bof"** — A uniquely French non-committal expression: "meh / not really / I don't care"

**"Quand même"** — All the same / anyway / nonetheless — very common filler

**"Voilà / Voilà voilà"** — There you go / exactly / that's it — used constantly as a conversation filler/conclusion

## 19. False Friends & Tricky Vocabulary

Words that look like English but mean something different:

| French | Looks like | Actual meaning |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| *actuellement* | actually | currently, at present |
| *éventuellement* | eventually | possibly, perhaps |
| *sensible* | sensible | sensitive |
| *sympathique* | sympathetic | nice, friendly |
| *large* | large | wide |
| *rester* | rest | to stay, remain |
| *assister à* | assist | to attend |
| *attendre* | attend | to wait for |
| *passer un examen* | pass an exam | to take an exam |
| *réussir un examen* | — | to pass an exam |
| *librairie* | library | bookshop |
| *bibliothèque* | — | library |
| *journée* | journey | (whole) day |
| *location* | location | rental |

### Savoir vs Connaître (both = "to know")

- **Savoir** — to know a fact, how to do something:  
  *Je sais qu'il vient.* / *Je sais nager.*
  
- **Connaître** — to know a person, place, or work:  
  *Je connais Paris.* / *Tu connais Marie ?*

### Pouvoir vs Savoir (both can translate "can")

- **Pouvoir** — to be able to (circumstances permit):  
  *Je ne peux pas venir ce soir.* (I can't come tonight — I'm busy)
  
- **Savoir** — to know how to (skill):  
  *Elle sait parler japonais.* (She can speak Japanese — she knows how)

### Partir vs Quitter vs Sortir (all = "to leave")

- **Partir** — to leave (a place, intransitive): *Il part demain.*
- **Quitter** — to leave (something/someone, transitive): *Il quitte Paris. / Il quitte sa femme.*
- **Sortir** — to go out: *On sort ce soir ?*
